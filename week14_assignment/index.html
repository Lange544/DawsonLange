Is "Project White Sands" Finally Half-Life 3?

For over a decade, the idea of Half-Life 3 has been the biggest meme in the gaming community. It’s the game that everyone wants, but Valve refuses to talk about. However, in August 2024, things changed. A new leak surfaced that gave fans arguably the most solid evidence we’ve seen in years that Gordon Freeman might finally be returning. It all started with a resume, a codename, and some very suspicious code.

The Spark: The Resume Leak

In early August 2024, the gaming internet went into a frenzy because of a voice actor named Natasha Chandel. On her public resume, she listed a project she was working on called "Project White Sands." That might sound like a generic military game, but the key detail was the employer: Valve.

Almost immediately after fans noticed this and started posting about it on Reddit and Twitter, Chandel’s website was flooded with traffic. Shortly after the news broke, the reference to "Project White Sands" was scrubbed from her resume. In the gaming industry, this is usually a huge red flag. If it was a mistake or a fake project, it likely would have been ignored. The fact that it was panic-deleted usually suggests that the leak was real, and Valve didn't want anyone knowing about it yet.

The Half-Life Connection: Why "White Sands"?

So, why does a project named "White Sands" automatically mean Half-Life? It’s not just wishful thinking; it’s about geography and lore.

Dataminers and lore experts quickly pointed out the connection to the real world. "Project White Sands" is almost certainly a reference to the White Sands Missile Range and National Park located in New Mexico.

If you’ve played the original Half-Life, you know that the Black Mesa Research Facility—the setting where everything goes wrong—is also located in the New Mexico desert. It seems like a very deliberate play on words by Valve: "White Sands" mirrors "Black Mesa." It’s exactly the kind of clever codename a developer would use to hint at the game's setting without giving the title away.

The Code: Evidence in the Engine

The evidence goes deeper than just a name. For a while now, dataminers (people who dig through game files to find hidden info) have been finding strange things inside Valve’s other games.

Because Counter-Strike 2 and Dota 2 both run on Valve's Source 2 engine, they often share underlying code. By digging into these files, miners found numerous references to a single-player project codenamed "HLX."

Found in the Files:

  • The HEV Suit: The iconic hazard suit worn by the main character, Gordon Freeman.
  • Gravity Manipulation: References to the Gravity Gun mechanics.
  • Xen Creatures: Assets related to the alien enemies from the series.

When you combine the "HLX" code findings with the "Project White Sands" resume leak, it paints a pretty clear picture. "HLX" is likely the technical codename, while "White Sands" is the internal project title.

What Comes Next?

To summarize, we have a credible leak from a voice actor, a codename that ties directly to the setting of the first game, and hard code evidence of Half-Life mechanics being tested in the Source 2 engine.

While Valve hasn't said a word, the timing feels significant. The gaming community is currently speculating that an official announcement could happen on December 11th at The Game Awards. This event is hosted by Geoff Keighley, who has a long history with Valve (he literally wrote the book on the making of the original games). If Valve is going to break the silence and finally announce Half-Life 3—or whatever this game turns out to be—that would be the place to do it.

For now, we just have to wait, but for the first time in a long time, the hype feels justified.
